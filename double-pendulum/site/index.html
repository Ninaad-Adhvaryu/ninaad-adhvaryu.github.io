If you change the shape of gravity, does chaos follow?

On what happens to a double pendulum when "downward" is no longer constant — and on what that reveals about the assumptions quietly built into every physical model.

Ninaad Adhvaryu DOP853 · Poincaré sections · Lyapunov exponents Complete

The double pendulum is physics's most reliable demonstration of classical chaos: two rods, two pivots, deterministic Newtonian mechanics — and within a few seconds of release, no trajectory can be predicted more than a moment ahead. Two initial conditions separated by a part in a million diverge exponentially. This is what chaos means, in a system where nothing is hidden.

What almost every treatment shares is an assumption so obvious it goes unstated: that gravity is uniform. The same g, pointing in the same direction, at every point the system occupies. For a pendulum in a lab, this is accurate to any precision you'd care about. But the assumption does something deeper than approximate a physical detail. It fixes the shape of the potential energy landscape before you've asked any questions about the dynamics. I wanted to know whether chaos belongs to the double pendulum itself — or to the specific field geometry under which it's usually studied.

Changing the field

The simplest modification is a linear gravity gradient: instead of g being constant, it varies linearly with height. The effective field experienced by each mass depends on where it sits in the pendulum's configuration. The equations of motion are only slightly more complex — the coupling between the two rods picks up additional terms, since the forces on the upper and lower masses now differ depending on instantaneous position — but the geometry of the potential energy landscape shifts. The gradient introduces a new parameter into the problem: its sign and magnitude.

The simulation below integrates the modified equations in real time and builds the Poincaré section as it runs. A point is recorded each time the upper rod crosses vertical (θ₁ = 0) with positive angular velocity. In a regular system, these points trace closed curves; in a chaotic one, they fill the section with an unstructured scatter. The dimensionless FTLE readout — the finite-time Lyapunov exponent normalised by √(g/L), so it can be compared across gravity models — measures how fast nearby trajectories separate.

What the gradient does — and doesn't do

At θ₁ = 120° with equal masses, uniform gravity produces full chaos within a few seconds: the Poincaré section fills with scattered points and the dimensionless FTLE settles around λ* ≈ 0.079. Switching to the gradient model at the same initial conditions produces something different. The section develops structure — curves rather than a dense scatter — and λ* drops to around 0.021. That is a three- to four-fold suppression of the chaotic divergence rate.

What surprised me was the non-monotonicity. The gradient doesn't simply suppress chaos in proportion to its magnitude. The relationship between gradient strength and the FTLE is non-linear, and the sign of the gradient matters: a field that weakens with height (dg/dy < 0, which is what the simulation implements) tends to suppress the chaotic sea at the configurations I examined, while a field that strengthens with height can produce the opposite. At some intermediate values, suppression is strongest; push the gradient further and the system finds its way back to erratic behaviour. The field geometry is acting as a hidden control parameter for chaos — one that the uniform-gravity model cannot represent at all, because it has no geometry to speak of.

The astrophysical cases

The more unusual test was to place the pendulum in a gravitational field generated by a specific compact object, using the Paczyński–Wiita pseudo-potential — a classical approximation that captures the key relativistic corrections near a compact mass without requiring full general relativity. I ran this for three cases: the Earth, the Sun, and Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the centre of our galaxy.

The naive expectation might be that the black hole case would produce the most striking deviation from uniform-gravity behaviour — after all, the field near Sgr A* is extreme. But the relevant quantity turned out not to be the mass of the central object. It was the dimensionless ratio of pendulum length to distance from the source, L/r. For the Earth and Sun cases, L/r is vanishingly small — the pendulum links are negligible compared to the distance from the centre of mass, and the field is effectively uniform across the entire system. The dynamics matched the standard uniform-gravity case almost exactly. For Sgr A* at a distance comparable to the pendulum's own scale, L/r approaches unity, the field is genuinely non-uniform across the system, and the dynamics diverge.

The lesson was geometric, not astrophysical. What mattered was the ratio of scales, not the exotic nature of the source. A black hole at a great distance is, dynamically, the same as any other mass at a great distance.

What this is really about

The deeper point — the one that motivated the project — is that the uniform gravity assumption isn't a neutral simplification. It pre-determines what behaviour the model is capable of exhibiting. A model built on uniform gravity will never show you configurations where the gradient's sign is a control parameter for chaos, because the model has no gradient. The assumption constrains the answer space before the question has been asked.

That's not a unique property of pendulums. Every physical model carries embedded assumptions about the environment it operates in, and those assumptions are usually invisible precisely because they're accurate for the typical use case. The double pendulum was useful here because it's simple enough that the modification is tractable, sensitive enough that the modification produces measurable effects, and well-studied enough that departures from the standard behaviour are legible. But the observation is more general: a model that works well in one field geometry may fail — not by producing wrong numbers, but by lacking the structure to produce the right question.
